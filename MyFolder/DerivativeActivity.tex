\section*{The Limit Definition of the Derivative}


Recall that \textbf{the slope of a curve at a point is equal to the slope of the tangent line at that point}.  The figure below shows the graph
of a function $f(x)$ and its tangent line at point $P (x, f(x)).$  To find the slope of the tangent line at $P$ using the slope formula, we need to 
know two points that lie on the line.  The tangent line and $f$ have point $P$ common, but we do not have enough information to determine the 
coordinates of a second point.  So, instead of finding the exact slope, let's approximate it using the slope of a secant line.  A \textbf{secant line} 
is a line that passes through two points on a curve.

The secant line in the graph above passes through the point $P$ and a nearby point $Q(x+\Delta x, f(x+\Delta x)).$  If $\Delta x$ is a very small number,
then the slope of the secant line will provide a good approximation to the slope of the tangent line.  The \textbf{slope of the secant line} is the 
\textbf{average rate of change} of $f$ on $[x, x+ \Delta x]$ and is given below.


\[ m_{\sec} = \frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}     \]


Notice that as $\Delta x \rightarrow 0,$ point $Q$ will get closer to point $P,$ and the secant line will start to look more like
the tangent line.  To see this, click and drag point $Q$ toward point $P$ in the interactive Desmos graph below.  If the left sidebar 
is showing math, click $\mathbf{\ll}$ to hide the contents.

Since the secant line approaches the tangent line as $\Delta x \rightarrow 0,$ we can find the \emph{exact} slope of the tangent line by taking the limit
of the slope of the secant line.

\[ m_{\tan} = \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}  \]

The \textbf{slope of the tangent line} is the \textbf{instantaneous rate of change} of $f$ at $P$ and is denoted by $f'(x).$

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}  \]

The limit $f'(x)$ is called the \textbf{derivative} of $f$ and gives the \emph{exact} slope of the curve $f$ at any point $x.$

\begin{theorem}[\textbf{Limit Definition
of the Derivative of a Function}]

~ \\[1em]

The \textbf{derivative} of a function $f(x)$ is

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} \]

provided that the limit exists.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
\textbf{Quotient Rule} If we think of the function in the numerator as the Hi function and the function in the denominator as the Lo function,
we can remember the Quotient Rule as
\[ \frac{d}{dx} \left[\frac{\textrm{Hi}}{\textrm{Lo}} \right]  = \frac{\textrm{HiD  Lo} - \textrm{LoD  Hi}}{(\textrm{Lo})^2}  \]

where HiD is the derivative of the Hi function and LoD is the derivative of the Lo function.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
\textbf{General Power Rule}
While the Chain Rule handles derivatives of all composite functions, the General Power Rule serves as a specialized shortcut for differentiating powers of functions
and is often easier to apply.
\end{remark}


\begin{theorem}[\textbf{Derivatives of the Natural Exponential and Logarithmic Functions}]


\[ \frac{d}{dx} [e^x ] = e^x \]

\[ \frac{d}{dx} [\ln x ] = \frac{1}{x} \]

If $u$ is a function of $x,$ then by the Chain rule

\[ \frac{d}{dx} [e^u ] = e^u \cdot u' \]

\[ \frac{d}{dx} [\ln u ] = \frac{1}{u} \cdot u'  \]

\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\textbf{Derivatives of Trigonometric Functions}]

\[
\begin{aligned}
    \frac{d}{dx} (\sin x) &= \cos x  & \hspace{2cm} \frac{d}{dx} (\cos x) &= -\sin x \\
    \frac{d}{dx} (\tan x) &= \sec^2 x & \hspace{2cm} \frac{d}{dx} (\cot x) &= -\csc^2 x \\
    \frac{d}{dx} (\sec x) &= \sec x \tan x & \hspace{2cm} \frac{d}{dx} (\csc x) &= -\csc x \cot x
\end{aligned}
\]

\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\textbf{Transcendental Functions and the Chain Rule}]

If $u$ is a function of $x,$ then

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[\sin u] = (\cos u) u'  \hspace{0.88in} \frac{d}{dx}[\cos u] = (-\sin u) u' \]
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[\tan u] = (\sec^2 u) u' \hspace{0.8in} \frac{d}{dx}[\cot u] = (-\csc^2 u) u' \]
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[\sec u] \hspace{0.01in} = \hspace{0.01in} (\sec u \tan u) u' \hspace{0.5in} \frac{d}{dx}[ \csc u] =
(-\csc u \cot u) u' \]
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[e^u] = e^u u' \hspace{0.8in} \frac{d}{dx} [\ln u] = \frac{u'}{u}  \hspace{0.8in} \frac{d}{dx} [  \ln |u|   ] = \frac{u'}{u} \]

\end{theorem}
